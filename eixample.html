A Journey Through Modernism

Eixample is infused with the spirit of this architectural movement, which has been carefully preserved and celebrated for over a century. Join us on a brief journey through time, from today's vibrant urban landscape back to the early days of Modernism.

1984

Eixample's significance as a cultural and architectural landmark was further solidified when parts of its grid, including the Sagrada Família, were included in the UNESCO World Heritage List. This acknowledgment highlighted the neighborhood's dedication to preserving its rich architectural heritage.

1930s

The neighborhood flourished as a hub for innovation and modern living. The grid layout, wide avenues, and chamfered corners envisioned by Ildefons Cerdà facilitated an inclusive urban environment, emphasizing community spaces and enhancing [re]sidents' quality of life.

1900s

Eixample became a canvas for Modernist architects like Antoni Gaudí, Lluís Domènech i Montaner, and Josep Puig i Cadafalch. Their imaginative designs turned the neighborhood into an open-air museum of Modernism, with structures that are still celebrated today for their creativity and elegance.

1860

The story of Eixample began with the Cerdà Plan, a visionary urban expansion project aimed at creating a healthier, more organized city. The plan introduced wide streets and open spaces, breaking away from the cramped medieval city center and setting the stage for Eixample’s emergence as a symbol of modernity and progressive living.
